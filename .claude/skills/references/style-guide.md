# BiteSizeAI Editorial Style Guide

This document defines the voice, tone, and writing philosophy for BiteSizeAI posts.

All writing generated by AI skills must follow these principles.

---

# Mission

BiteSizeAI exists to make modern AI concepts understandable to curious engineers, builders, and non technical readers alike.

The goal is not to impress readers with complexity.

The goal is to help readers feel:

"I finally understand this."

"This is intuitive."

Posts should emphasize **intuition, mental models, and practical understanding** over mathematical formality.

---

# Audience

Primary audience:

Curious technical and non-technical readers who are interested in AI but may not have deep ML backgrounds.

Typical readers include:

- software engineers
- curious tinkerers
- startup builders
- technically curious professionals
- developers exploring AI tools

Assume readers are intelligent but unfamiliar with some AI terminology.

---

# Voice and Tone

The tone should be:

- calm
- thoughtful
- intelligent
- grounded
- non-hypey

It should feel like a **clear explanation from a thoughtful engineer**, not a marketing article.

Avoid sounding like:

- a research paper
- a startup pitch deck
- a viral Twitter thread
- a hype-driven AI blog

---

# Core Writing Principles

## 1. Intuition First

Explain the concept intuitively before explaining mechanics.

Bad:

> A vector embedding is a numerical representation of semantic relationships.

Better:

> An embedding is a way of placing ideas in space so that similar things end up near each other.

---

## 2. Simple Language

Prefer simple explanations over technical language.

Use technical terms only when necessary.

When using technical terms, define them lightly.

---

## 3. Short Paragraphs

Posts should feel readable and relaxed.

Avoid large dense paragraphs.

---

## 4. Use Analogies

Analogies help readers build mental models.

Examples:

- libraries
- maps
- physical space
- neighborhoods
- search
- recommendation systems

---

## 5. Concrete Examples

When possible, explain how the concept shows up in real systems.

Examples:

- RAG pipelines
- search
- recommendation engines
- AI assistants

---

# Preferred Post Structure

Most posts should follow this structure:

1. Hook — introduce the idea or confusion
2. The Question — what people struggle to understand
3. The Intuition — explain the idea simply
4. Analogy — a concrete mental model
5. Mechanism — how it works at a high level
6. Example — how it appears in real systems
7. Why It Matters
8. Closing Insight

---

# Visual Thinking

BiteSizeAI posts should often suggest visuals.

Helpful visual ideas include:

- clusters in space
- diagrams of system flow
- simplified architecture diagrams
- before/after comparisons

---

# What To Avoid

Do NOT write content that sounds like:

AI hype blogs:

> This revolutionary breakthrough will change everything.

Startup marketing:

> Unlock the power of AI-driven transformation.

Academic writing:

> In this article we explore the mathematical formalization of...

Overly technical explanations when intuition would suffice.

## Also Avoid
- em dashes
- contrastive negation
- Formulaic Structure & Tone: Often robotic, overly formal, and structured with repetitive, predictable sentence lengths.
- Overused "AI Phrases": Frequent use of buzzwords like "transform," "unlock," "empower," "delve," "leverage," and "landscape," as noted on Grammarly's blog.
- Repetitive Content: AI often rephrases the same points multiple times without adding new information.
- Lack of Genuine Depth: While grammatical, the text often lacks original insight, personal experiences, or a unique, human voice.
- Specific Structure Elements: A preference for structured lists, bolded headers, and, in some cases, excessive emojis.
- Overly Prolific/Polite: AI tends to be diplomatic and frequently includes a concluding "summary" section that restates the premise.
- Specific LLM Preferences:
 - ChatGPT: Often uses "not only... but also," and "in conclusion".
 - Claude: Tends to use "Based on," "Here is," and "In summary".
 - Gemini: Frequently utilizes "but also," "helps in," and "might"

---

# Ending Philosophy

Strong BiteSizeAI posts end with a simple takeaway.

Example endings:

- a reframing of the concept
- a simple rule of thumb
- how the concept connects to real systems
- a mental model the reader can remember

---

# Tone Checklist

Before finalizing content, ask:

Does this feel:

- clear?
- calm?
- intuitive?
- grounded?

Or does it feel:

- hypey?
- dense?
- academic?

If the latter, simplify.
